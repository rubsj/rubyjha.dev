## Why an Engineering Manager Is Building AI Systems

I have spent 20+ years building enterprise platforms and leading engineering teams — 7+ of those years in people management. At State Street, I own platform strategy for a Self-Service Portal serving 40+ enterprise customers, driving $250K/month in operational cost savings and a 60% reduction in support demand. At EY, I ran a 12-engineer global organization across the US, India, and Poland. At Centene, I led modernization of a healthcare platform covering approximately 44 million lives.

That is a solid engineering leadership track record. But I noticed something shifting in the industry: the next generation of engineering leaders needs to be AI-literate, not just AI-adjacent. Reviewing PRs from ML engineers isn't enough. Approving infrastructure budgets for GPU clusters isn't enough. I wanted to understand the systems — how RAG pipelines actually retrieve and rank, why fine-tuning sometimes fails silently, what multi-agent orchestration looks like when agents disagree.

So I decided to build. Not take courses. Not watch tutorials. Build production-grade AI systems with real evaluation metrics, honest tradeoff documentation, and architecture decisions I can defend in a technical interview.

## The Portfolio Architecture

These aren't random projects stitched together to fill a GitHub profile. They follow a deliberate progression through the applied AI stack:

1. **Data Generation** (P1) — Schema-driven synthetic data with Pydantic validation and LLM-as-Judge quality scoring
2. **Evaluation** (P2) — Multi-strategy RAG evaluation comparing 15 vector configurations across chunking, embeddings, and reranking
3. **Fine-Tuning** (P3) — Contrastive embedding fine-tuning: standard vs LoRA, head-to-head with comprehensive metrics
4. **Applied RAG** (P4-P5) — AI Resume Coach and a production RAG pipeline with hybrid search and FastAPI endpoints
5. **Multi-Agent Systems** (P6-P9) — Feedback intelligence, sprint planning, digital clone, and DevOps RCA — all using CrewAI orchestration

Each project builds on the previous. P1 generates data that could train models evaluated by P2's framework. P3's fine-tuned embeddings feed into P5's production pipeline. P6-P9 all use multi-agent patterns that emerge naturally once you understand the single-agent limitations from P4-P5.

**P1: Synthetic Data Generation** took 14 hours. The pipeline generates schema-driven records using GPT-4o-mini, then validates them with a GPT-4o LLM-as-Judge. The interesting finding wasn't the 100% generation success rate — it was calibrating the judge. My first judge prompt approved everything (useless). After four iterations, I hit a 20% failure rate that matched my manual assessment. The correction loop then reduced failures by 78%.

**P2: RAG Evaluation Pipeline** took 19 hours. I tested 15 vector configurations — every combination of chunking strategy, embedding model, and reranking. The best config (semantic chunking + OpenAI embeddings + Cohere reranking) hit Recall@5 of 0.747, beating the BM25 keyword baseline by 96%. But the most important finding was a faithfulness score of only 0.511 — meaning the LLM hallucinated almost half the time even when the retriever found the right chunks. Good retrieval is necessary but not sufficient. The project ships with 384+ tests at 95% coverage.

**P3: Contrastive Embedding Fine-Tuning** took 12 hours. I ran a head-to-head comparison: standard fine-tuning (all 22.7M parameters) vs LoRA (73K parameters — 0.32% of the model). LoRA achieved 96.9% of standard performance with a 309x smaller model. The hidden lesson: LoRA needs a 10x higher learning rate than standard fine-tuning. My first attempt with the default learning rate produced garbage — and I would have wrongly concluded LoRA doesn't work for sentence transformers.

## What "Production-Grade" Means to Me

Every tutorial on the internet will show you how to build a RAG pipeline in 20 lines of LangChain. That's not what these projects are. Production-grade means:

- **Evaluation frameworks** — not just "it works," but measured against baselines with reproducible metrics
- **Architecture Decision Records** — every major "why" is documented, including the paths I didn't take
- **Error handling** — what happens when the LLM returns garbage, when the vector store is empty, when the API rate-limits you
- **Real test coverage** — P2 has 384+ tests. P3 has 124 tests at 94% coverage. Not afterthoughts — these were written as part of the build
- **Deployment considerations** — containerized where appropriate, CLI interfaces for reproducibility, structured logging

<Callout type="tip">
  The easiest way to spot a tutorial project vs a production system: ask "what happens when it fails?" Tutorial projects
  don't have an answer. Production systems have error handling, retry logic, and degradation strategies documented in
  ADRs.
</Callout>
